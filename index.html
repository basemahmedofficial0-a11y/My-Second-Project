The Art of Software Craft

Basem Ahmed · March 10, 2026

The debate around programming as a design tool is a recurring discussion, and it has flared up once again. I have thought about this deeply. Design as an act of exploration, not just a production line. How tools carry their own biases, and how those perspectives shape what we consider "logical" to attempt. Why constraints that appear too early can shut the door on creative possibilities before you even have a chance to discover them. I am skeptical of the industry’s push toward total unification, reducing a complex human process into mere lines of code and calling it progress.

Recent discourse has focused on whether designers should learn to code, if the browser is the right medium for creativity, and how code reveals the "ultimate truth" of a product, and so on. But I believe centering this debate on tools and languages is a reduction of the true essence of the creative process.

The bigger question for me is what happens to our perception of a developer's contribution in the future, especially now with the rise of AI agents and these revolutionary tools. How do professional roles evolve, and what should we start expecting from each specialty in this constantly shifting technological environment?

Have developers now become experience engineers, with the same technical expectations? Do stakeholders start having the same expectations of designers? Will we still need titles like "Head of Engineering" and "Head of Design," or does it transform into something like "Head of Craft"? Titles do not matter much, but these questions are useful because they reveal what we truly value and how we believe tasks are effectively accomplished.

Then comes the practical question. If everyone is pushed toward direct engineering, designing with code immediately, is that a healthy direction? What might we lose in terms of creative imagination or gain in terms of execution speed and productivity?

Part of why this discussion gets messy is that the word "design" means different things to different people. Your view of it depends on the type of work you do, and how you understand the practice of innovation as either a manual or technical craft.

I believe creativity comes in many flavors. It is influenced by the individual, the domain, the market, and the nature of the users. In consumer products, you might need to test ideas quickly because motivations are hard to predict. In B2B or enterprise systems, you often have more context and can build based on that. Some industries require extreme reliability and clarity. The environment matters too; stakeholders, company culture, and your personal skills. If you lean toward visuals, you lead with visuals. If you are strong in code, you use it early.

So there is a very broad domain for building software, with diverse tools and methods within it. The argument of "Code vs. No-code" often misses the point and becomes a polarizing force rather than a driving one.

What I am trying to say is that I want to elevate this discussion above the level of tools, and ensure that tools do not dominate the future of creativity. I do not want us to devalue conceptual and divergent thinking just because new tools make the execution process easier and faster than ever before.

Even engineers who work with code all day step away from it occasionally. They draw architectures, plan systems, and think about technical tradeoffs. While code is where software lives, it is not always the place where all critical product decisions should be made.

To explain what I mean, I have to start with how I understand the building process and how I have practiced it over the past years in developing digital projects.

Engineering the Problem

First of all, I am not a traditional academic developer. I am self-taught through hundreds of software projects over the last two decades. So I am not advocating for gatekeeping or a single "right" way to build. But I do believe innovation is rarely linear. You work at different levels of abstraction and move between them. While the outcome is the goal, spending time on the journey can make the final result much better.

Through this work, I learned to question the problem first, and not treat it as a given assumption. If asked to work on something, I start with: Is this a real problem? What if we do not do this? Who defined the dimensions of this problem in the first place?

It sounds philosophical, but what I have found is that the most common reason projects stall or fail is that the problem was not clear. People will not agree on solutions because they have different problems in mind. The solution becomes a compromise for many issues, rather than a clean solution to one major one. This compounds when you have too many stakeholders involved.

To tackle this, I would do two things. Write the problem as I understood it, and ask who the actual stakeholders are. Then, when presenting solutions, I would repeat the problem definition to see the reactions. If there was an objection, I would stop and get everyone to align on the problem. So what does this have to do with design or code? This sounds like pure corporate talk.

You will always face stakeholders; if not colleagues, then customers. You have to understand whether the criticism is genuine because the solution is not good, or because people do not agree on what the problem is. Sometimes you build a solution for the wrong problem entirely. The problem is the first part of the equation, the foundation for everything you try to design and build later with code.

In my recent project, we quickly realized that users needed to manage their tasks. So the roadmap was "build a task system." Everyone agreed. But if you think deeper, why do people need to organize work into tasks? Why not just work on what appears before them? What happens if they do not have a system at all?

You can dive deep into project management; there is a whole discipline behind it and many ways to apply it. But if your vision is to build a purpose-built tool for software companies, you start narrowing the scope. What do they actually care about in their crowded technical day?

Simply put, tasks are a natural abstraction for companies to manage workflows. They help with accountability, ownership, cross-team collaboration, predictability of results, and visibility for both managers and employees alike.

Now you might ask why research something so basic or well-understood. Sometimes you do not need to. But learning the landscape and the history of previous solutions gives you the right orientation. You find assumptions you can update, or you decide which technical traditions you must follow to ensure quality.

Most importantly, learning the problem first helps you decide what you want to do about it. What direction the product vision is pulling you toward. What you want to radically improve and influence to serve the end user.

In our vision, we saw that the "Project" might be the most important unit of work. Products are made of them, organizations orient around them, and you celebrate when one is completed. That understanding gave us a clear direction for the problem we were actually trying to solve with code.

Engineering the Solution

I think of engineering the solution in two stages: The conceptual stage and the execution stage. The conceptual stage is finding the overall form the solution will take. While the execution stage is building it and bringing it to life on the screen for users.

Take, for example, project management in a tracking tool; you could treat it as a large task with sub-tasks, a simple folder, or an entirely independent entity. These are conceptual ideas you can consider without writing a single line of code. The decision depends on your understanding of users, the company, and the vision you want. All of these concepts can succeed in different contexts.

Because we saw projects as a significant entity, different from simple tasks, they had to have their own shape. A project must have clear functions that communicate status, reasoning, user feedback, and timing. Demoting projects to just a "label" or a random collection did not seem like the right decision.

This conceptual stage might happen in words, on paper, in a design tool, or even in code drafts. It is about giving those ideas a shape, trying different directions, and choosing the one that serves the problem and the product vision best.

Once I have a direction I believe in, it is ready for broader discussion. You test the concept by starting the actual building process and turning imagination into a tangible reality.

It is the part closest to the final output. You make the solution concept actually work, shaping its true features. Code, or dealing with the raw material of the product, is essential for this. And there are times when you gain new insights and have to go back to review the problem or the concept again.

There are different ways to use code in this stage. You can build experimental tools or write "throwaway" code for testing. This removes some of the constraints of production code and makes the process more exploratory. My point is that while you might see the execution stage as the "real" part, you have already built a foundation of context and confidence before it.

Think of it in AI terms. The work you do before the code is the goal, the context, and the "prompt" you build for the agent to address the problem. The execution work is where you guide the agent through specific decisions to reach the result.

After reading all this, you might think, who has time for this? We need to ship and launch. Why not build right away and figure it out as we go? I do not think it is about time. My explanation might have been long, but the process itself can be very fast if you are used to it.

My feeling is that without this pre-built context and goal, you might be moving toward a direction, but not one that was chosen intentionally, which might lead to rework later.

This brings me back to where I started. Our industry is not very patient, and once you start building solutions directly for production as a default, the cultural reasons for considering problems and intentions start to fade. We begin devaluing the "why" behind our work in favor of increasing "output."

My worry is not the code or the tools themselves. It is a decline in deep thinking, and with that, a decline in unique, well-crafted products. The question is how we keep that alive even as new technologies and smart agents emerge in our world.

To me, building was never about the shape of the button or what it does, or which programming language you use. It was and still is about finding the right problem, the right intent, and the right vision. The feature you build today should be a considered step toward that grander vision.

Thank you to all the colleagues and developers who contributed their thoughts and discussions on this important topic for the future of the software industry.
